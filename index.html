FAQ

Common Questions

Doomscrolling is the compulsive habit of endlessly scrolling through negative, distressing, or simply mindless content — news feeds, social media, short videos — even when it makes you feel worse. Your brain gets a low-grade dopamine hit from each new piece of content, which keeps you hooked even when the content itself is stressful or meaningless.
The global average is 2 hours 27 minutes per day on social media (DataReportal 2025). Across a 50-year period, that adds up to roughly 7 years of waking life spent scrolling. TikTok users average 95 minutes per day on that single app alone — more than 24 full days per year.
Yes — the research is consistent. Doomscrolling is linked to increased anxiety and depression, disrupted sleep, shortened attention spans, elevated cortisol levels, and a general sense of time-wasting and regret. Multiple studies have found that people who report higher social media use also report lower life satisfaction.
Yes — but not by willpower alone. The apps are engineered to maximize your time on screen. You need friction on the side of mindlessness and ease on the side of intention: app timers, grayscale mode, removing apps from your home screen, charging your phone outside the bedroom. Research shows even a 30% reduction in scroll time significantly improves mood and sleep within 2 weeks.
It's a reflective tool, not a clinical measurement. It takes your self-reported daily scroll time and multiplies it across your years of habit. The goal isn't precision — it's perspective. People tend to underestimate their screen time, so your real number is likely higher than what the calculator shows.
Intentional use has a purpose: catching up with specific people, sharing something, getting specific information. Doomscrolling is characterized by the absence of intent — you open an app because your brain craved stimulation. The test: before you open an app, do you know what you're going there to do? If not, it's probably doomscrolling.
